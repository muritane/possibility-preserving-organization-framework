# Geometric Possibility-Preserving Organization Framework

## Viability Regions, Organizational Trajectories, and Open-Ended Accumulation

---

# Abstract

Complexity accumulates through the preservation, reuse, and composition of organizational structures that maintain useful future possibilities.

A persistent abstraction is not merely a structure.

A persistent abstraction is a constrained organizational trajectory that remains within an admissible possibility region generated by supporting structures.

Support structures create viability regions.

Organizational states move within those regions.

Maintenance preserves trajectories within viable boundaries.

Affordances correspond to future trajectories that remain viable.

Accumulation occurs when viability-preserving organizational structures and possibility regions are retained, reused, and expanded faster than they are destroyed.

The central quantity of the framework is organizational viability.

Viability measures the distance of an organizational trajectory from organizational failure.

---

# Part I: Primitive Geometric Objects

## 1. Identity

A single point defines identity.

```text
p
```

Identity alone contains no relation.

No organization exists.

---

## 2. Relation

Two distinct points create a relation.

```text
p₁ , p₂
```

Relations introduce:

* distance
* direction
* interval
* ordering
* constraint

Organization begins with relations.

---

## 3. Constraint Regions

Multiple relations create constrained regions.

Examples:

* intervals
* polygons
* manifolds
* feasible solution spaces
* organizational possibility spaces

Constraints create an interior.

Organization requires admissible regions rather than isolated points.

---

## 4. Support Structures

Support structures generate admissible organizational regions.

Examples:

* skeletal systems
* metabolic systems
* infrastructure
* institutions
* software dependencies
* legal systems

Support structures do not constitute organization themselves.

Support structures create regions within which organization may persist.

---

## 5. Viability Region

Each abstraction possesses a viability region

```text
P_i
```

representing admissible organizational states.

The viability region is generated by:

* dependencies
* interfaces
* resources
* constraints
* support structures

States outside the region are organizationally nonviable.

---

## 6. Organizational State

Each abstraction possesses an organizational state

```text
x_i(t)
```

The organizational state evolves through time.

Examples:

* center of mass of a robot
* metabolic state of a cell
* operational state of an institution
* execution state of software
* knowledge state of a civilization

---

## 7. Organizational Trajectory

Organization is represented by a trajectory

```text
x_i(t)
```

moving through a viability region.

Persistence corresponds to continued existence of viable trajectories.

Organization is therefore dynamic rather than static.

---

## 8. Failure Boundary

The boundary of the viability region is

```text
∂P_i
```

Crossing the boundary constitutes organizational failure.

Examples:

* robot falls
* organism dies
* institution collapses
* software becomes unusable
* language becomes extinct

---

# Part II: Persistent Abstractions

## 9. Persistent Abstraction

A persistent abstraction is

```text
A_i =
(P_i,
x_i(t),
I_i,
D_i,
F_i)
```

where

```text
P_i
```

is the viability region,

```text
x_i(t)
```

is the organizational trajectory,

```text
I_i
```

are interfaces,

```text
D_i
```

are dependencies,

```text
F_i
```

are future affordances.

---

## 10. Persistence

Persistence occurs when

```text
x_i(t)
∈ P_i
```

for sufficient duration.

---

## 11. Stabilization

An abstraction stabilizes when its trajectory remains within its viability region despite perturbation.

---

# Part III: Viability Geometry

## 12. Viability

Viability measures distance from failure.

```text
V_i
=
distance(
x_i(t),
∂P_i
)
```

Higher viability implies greater resilience against perturbation.

---

## 13. Viability Reserve

Let

```text
T_i
```

represent the minimum viability threshold.

Then

```text
B_i
=
V_i - T_i
```

defines viability reserve.

Viability reserve measures available resilience capacity.

---

## 14. Collapse Tolerance

Let

```text
τ_i
```

represent allowable duration below threshold.

Collapse occurs when

```text
V_i < T_i
```

for longer than

```text
τ_i
```

---

## 15. Maintenance

Maintenance consists of actions that preserve trajectories inside viability regions.

```text
M_i
```

Examples:

* repair
* regulation
* homeostasis
* governance
* feedback control
* memory preservation

Maintenance continually redirects trajectories away from failure boundaries.

---

## 16. Degradation

Degradation consists of processes that move trajectories toward failure boundaries.

```text
D_i
```

Examples:

* entropy
* wear
* corruption
* institutional decay
* resource depletion

---

# Part IV: Affordances

## 17. Affordances

Affordances are viable future trajectories.

```text
F_i
```

Formally:

```text
F_i
=
{
future trajectories
remaining inside P_i
}
```

Affordances represent preserved future possibilities.

---

## 18. Affordance Capacity

Affordance capacity measures the volume of viable future trajectories available from the current state.

Large viability regions tend to preserve larger affordance spaces.

---

## 19. Reachability

```text
R_i
```

Measures whether an abstraction can be discovered, accessed, referenced, or utilized.

An affordance that cannot be reached is effectively lost.

---

# Part V: Support Geometry

## 20. Dependency Network

Dependencies form support networks.

```text
G=(A,E)
```

Each abstraction contributes to viability regions of dependent abstractions.

---

## 21. Support Capacity

```text
S_i
```

Measures viability support provided by supporting abstractions.

---

## 22. Support Burden

```text
C_i
```

Measures organizational resources consumed maintaining support structures.

Examples:

* energy
* computation
* administration
* maintenance
* coordination
* memory

---

## 23. Support Efficiency

```text
E_i
=
S_i / C_i
```

Higher efficiency permits larger viability regions at lower maintenance cost.

---

## 24. Support Compression

Support compression occurs when one support structure generates viability regions for many abstractions simultaneously.

Examples:

* DNA
* language
* mathematics
* operating systems
* legal frameworks

Support compression increases accumulation potential.

---

# Part VI: Generative Capacity

## 25. Generative Capacity

```text
G_i
```

Measures ability to generate new viable regions and trajectories.

Generative capacity differs from affordance capacity.

Affordance capacity preserves possibilities.

Generative capacity creates new possibilities.

---

## 26. Possibility Expansion

Possibility expansion occurs when new viable trajectories become reachable.

```text
ΔF_i > 0
```

---

## 27. Constraint-Satisfying Generation

Only trajectories satisfying all relevant constraints contribute to accumulation.

Generation without viability does not accumulate.

---

# Part VII: Composition

## 28. Reuse

```text
λ_i
```

Measures participation in future compositions.

---

## 29. Composition

New abstractions emerge through combining existing viability regions and trajectories.

---

## 30. Layer Formation

A new layer emerges when stabilized abstractions become reusable supports for future abstractions.

---

## 31. Recursive Support

Persistent abstractions become support structures for future abstractions.

```text
retained organization
→ support region generation
→ future organization
```

Accumulation is recursively self-supporting.

---

# Part VIII: Alternative Implementations

## 32. Implementation Independence

An abstraction is distinct from any particular implementation.

The trajectory may migrate between supporting substrates.

---

## 33. Migration

Migration transfers trajectories to new support structures.

Examples:

* oral tradition → writing
* paper archive → digital archive
* software → replacement hardware

Migration preserves viability.

---

## 34. Redundancy

Multiple implementations may generate overlapping viability regions.

Redundancy increases viability reserve.

---

```text
=
```


Support depends upon viability of supporting abstractions.

---

## 37. Persistence Condition

Persistence tends to occur when

```text
V_i > T_i
```

# Part XI: Structural Principles

## Principle 1: Viability Region Principle

Support structures generate viability regions.

Organization exists only within such regions.

---

## Principle 2: Trajectory Principle

Organization is a trajectory within a viability region rather than a static structure.

---

## Principle 3: Affordance Principle

Affordances are viable future trajectories.

Persistence without affordances contributes little accumulation.

---

## Principle 4: Maintenance Principle

Maintenance preserves trajectories within viability regions.

---

## Principle 5: Dependency Principle

Viability regions depend upon supporting abstractions.

Support failures shrink or destroy viability regions.

---

## Principle 6: Support Compression Principle

Support structures that generate large viability regions with low maintenance burden increase accumulation potential.

---

## Principle 7: Recursive Support Principle

Persistent abstractions become generators of future viability regions.

---

## Principle 8: Possibility Expansion Principle

Open-ended accumulation requires creation of viable future trajectories faster than viable trajectories are destroyed.

---

## Principle 9: Open-Ended Accumulation Constraint

Open-ended accumulation requires simultaneous preservation of:

* viability regions
* organizational trajectories
* support efficiency
* reachability
* interface integrity
* affordance capacity
* generative capacity
* reuse

across all required supporting layers.

---

# General Dynamic Principle

Support structures generate viability regions.

Organizational states move through those regions as trajectories.

Viability measures distance from organizational failure.

Affordances are viable future trajectories.

Maintenance preserves trajectories within viability regions.

Accumulation occurs when viability-preserving trajectories, support structures, and future possibility regions are retained, reused, composed, and expanded faster than they are destroyed.

Open-ended accumulation requires continual expansion of viable future possibility space while maintaining bounded support burden across all interdependent layers.
