# Possibility-Preserving Organization Framework

## Viability, Support, Affordances, and Open-Ended Organizational Accumulation

---

# Abstract

Complexity accumulates through the persistence, reuse, and composition of organizational structures that preserve useful future possibilities.

A persistent abstraction is an organizational structure that remains identifiable through time, constrains behavior into a viable region of state-space, and preserves a space of admissible future interactions.

Accumulation occurs when possibility-preserving abstractions are retained, reused, and composed faster than organizational structure and future possibilities are lost.

The central quantity of the framework is organizational viability.

Viability measures the ability of an abstraction to remain within admissible organizational boundaries despite degradation and environmental change.

Open-ended accumulation requires simultaneous preservation of:

* viability
* support capacity
* interface integrity
* reachability
* affordance capacity
* generative capacity
* reuse

across multiple interdependent organizational layers.

---

# Part I: Primitive Objects

## 1. Persistent Abstraction

A persistent abstraction is an organizational structure

```text
A = (S,T,I,D,F)
```

where

```text
S = admissible states
T = admissible transitions
I = interfaces
D = dependencies
F = future affordances
```

Persistent abstractions preserve recognizable identity through time despite lower-level change.

Examples:

* atoms
* cells
* organisms
* persons
* institutions
* languages
* software systems
* scientific theories

---

## 2. State-Space

Every abstraction possesses an admissible state-space.

```text
S*
```

An abstraction remains viable only while its state remains within admissible boundaries.

Crossing these boundaries constitutes organizational failure.

---

## 3. Transition Structure

```text
T ⊆ S × S
```

Organization consists of constraints on both states and transitions.

---

## 4. Interfaces

Interfaces specify permissible interactions between abstractions.

Large-scale composition requires interface preservation.

---

## 5. Dependencies

Dependencies form support networks.

```text
G = (A,E)
```

Each abstraction depends upon supporting abstractions.

---

## 6. Affordances

Affordances are admissible future actions, interactions, or compositions enabled by an abstraction.

```text
F_i
```

A house preserves habitable possibilities.

A language preserves expressive possibilities.

A cell preserves biochemical possibilities.

Persistence without affordances contributes little organizational accumulation.

# Part II: Organizational Quantities

## 7. Viability

```text
V_i
```

Viability measures distance from organizational failure.

Operationally:

```text
V_i
=
distance(current state,
nearest failure boundary)
```

Higher viability implies greater resilience against perturbation.

---

## 8. Viability Reserve

```text
B_i
=
V_i - T_i
```

where

```text
T_i
```

is the persistence threshold.

Viability reserve measures resilience capacity.

---

## 9. Reachability

```text
R_i
```

Reachability measures whether an abstraction can be accessed, discovered, referenced, or utilized through valid organizational paths.

Knowledge that cannot be found is effectively lost.

---

## 10. Interface Integrity

```text
I_i
```

Measures preservation of interface semantics.

An abstraction may survive structurally while becoming unusable if interfaces degrade.

---

## 11. Maintenance

```text
M_i
```

Processes preserving organizational structure.

Examples:

* repair
* governance
* error correction
* memory preservation
* infrastructure upkeep

---

## 12. Degradation

```text
D_i
```

Processes reducing organizational structure.

Examples:

* entropy
* wear
* corruption
* institutional decay
* information loss

---

## 13. Dependency Support

```text
S_i
```

Support provided by supporting abstractions.

# Part III: Support Dynamics

## 14. Support Burden

```text
C_i
```

Support burden is organizational capacity consumed maintaining support structures.

Examples:

* energy
* mass
* repair
* coordination
* computation
* administration
* memory

Support is never free.

---

## 15. Support Efficiency

```text
E_i
=
S_i / C_i
```

Support efficiency measures support delivered per unit burden.

---

## 16. Minimum Sufficient Support Principle

Support structures exist to preserve future possibilities.

Excess support consumes capacity.

Insufficient support causes collapse.

Optimal support is typically neither maximal nor minimal.

Open-ended accumulation tends toward minimum sufficient support.

Examples:

* skeletal structures
* vascular networks
* software abstractions
* institutions

---

## 17. Support Compression

Support compression occurs when one support structure sustains many dependent abstractions while requiring little additional maintenance.

Examples:

* DNA
* language
* mathematics
* operating systems
* legal frameworks

Support compression increases accumulation potential.

---

## 18. Recursive Support

Persistent abstractions become support structures for future abstractions.

```text
retained organization
→ future support
```

Accumulation is recursively self-supporting.

---

# Part IV: Generative Capacity

## 19. Generative Capacity

```text
G_i
```

Generative capacity measures the volume of viable organizational configurations producible from an abstraction.

Examples:

* stem cells
* genomes
* languages
* programming languages
* mathematical formalisms

Generative capacity differs from persistence.

---

## 20. Constraint-Satisfying Generation

Generation is not the production of arbitrary structure.

Generation is the production of structure satisfying multiple simultaneous constraints.

Examples:

* developmental constraints
* energetic constraints
* spatial constraints
* informational constraints
* mechanical constraints

Only constraint-satisfying generation contributes to accumulation.

---

## 21. Possibility Expansion

An abstraction expands possibility space when it increases the number of viable future organizational configurations.

High generative capacity promotes accumulation.

# Part XI: Structural Theorems

## Theorem 1: Persistence Requirement

Only retained abstractions can participate in future composition.

---

## Theorem 2: Affordance Requirement

Persistence alone does not imply accumulation.

Abstractions must preserve future possibilities.

A diamond may persist indefinitely while contributing little accumulation.

---

## Theorem 3: Reuse Requirement

Retention without reuse contributes little long-term accumulation.

---

## Theorem 4: Dependency Constraint

Without viable support structures, dependent abstractions eventually lose viability.

---

## Theorem 5: Maintenance Constraint

If maintenance demand grows faster than maintenance capacity, accumulation eventually becomes negative.

---

## Theorem 6: Migration Advantage

Implementation-independent abstractions possess greater persistence potential.

---

## Theorem 7: Interface Constraint

Large-scale composition requires interface preservation.

---

## Theorem 8: Support Burden Constraint

Support structures consume organizational capacity.

If support burden grows faster than support efficiency, viability reserves decline.

---

## Theorem 9: Support Compression Principle

Support structures capable of sustaining many abstractions with low additional burden increase accumulation potential.

---

## Theorem 10: Recursive Support Principle

Persistent abstractions become future support structures.

Accumulation therefore increases both future capacity and future maintenance requirements.

---

## Theorem 11: Minimum Sufficient Support Principle

Support structures that maximize support often reduce future possibilities.

Open-ended accumulation requires support structures that preserve possibility space while consuming bounded capacity.

---

## Theorem 12: Generative Capacity Principle

Abstractions possessing high generative capacity contribute disproportionately to long-term accumulation.

Examples include:

* genomes
* stem cells
* languages
* mathematics
* software platforms

---

## Theorem 13: Possibility Expansion Principle

Open-ended accumulation requires the creation of viable future possibilities faster than possibilities are destroyed.

---

## Theorem 14: Open-Ended Accumulation Constraint

Open-ended accumulation requires simultaneous preservation of:

* viability
* support efficiency
* reachability
* interface integrity
* affordance capacity
* generative capacity
* reuse

across all necessary supporting layers.

---

# General Dynamic Principle

Persistent abstractions are organizational structures that preserve constrained but useful regions of future possibility space.

Viability measures distance from organizational failure.

Support structures preserve possibility spaces while consuming organizational capacity.

Accumulation occurs when possibility-preserving abstractions are retained, reused, composed, and generated faster than organizational structure and future possibilities are lost.

Open-ended accumulation requires support efficiency and generative capacity to grow sufficiently that future possibility space expands faster than support burden across all interdependent organizational layers.
