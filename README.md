# Geometric Possibility-Preserving Organization Framework

## Viability Regions, Organizational Trajectories, and Open-Ended Accumulation

---

# Abstract

Complexity accumulates through the preservation, reuse, and composition of organizational structures that maintain useful future possibilities.

A persistent abstraction is not merely a structure.

A persistent abstraction is a constrained organizational trajectory that remains within an admissible possibility region generated by supporting structures.

Support structures create viability regions.

Organizational states move within those regions.

Maintenance preserves trajectories within viable boundaries.

Affordances correspond to future trajectories that remain viable.

Accumulation occurs when viability-preserving organizational structures and possibility regions are retained, reused, and expanded faster than they are destroyed.

The central quantity of the framework is organizational viability.

Viability measures the distance of an organizational trajectory from organizational failure.

---

# Part I: Primitive Geometric Objects

## 1. Identity

A single point defines identity.

```text
p
```

Identity alone contains no relation.

No organization exists.

---

## 2. Relation

Two distinct points create a relation.

```text
p₁ , p₂
```

Relations introduce:

* distance
* direction
* interval
* ordering
* constraint

Organization begins with relations.

---

## 3. Constraint Regions

Multiple relations create constrained regions.

Examples:

* intervals
* polygons
* manifolds
* feasible solution spaces
* organizational possibility spaces

Constraints create an interior.

Organization requires admissible regions rather than isolated points.

---

## 4. Support Structures

Support structures generate admissible organizational regions.

Examples:

* skeletal systems
* metabolic systems
* infrastructure
* institutions
* software dependencies
* legal systems

Support structures do not constitute organization themselves.

Support structures create regions within which organization may persist.

---

## 5. Viability Region

Each abstraction possesses a viability region

```text
P_i
```

representing admissible organizational states.

The viability region is generated by:

* dependencies
* interfaces
* resources
* constraints
* support structures

States outside the region are organizationally nonviable.

---

## 6. Organizational State

Each abstraction possesses an organizational state

```text
x_i(t)
```

The organizational state evolves through time.

Examples:

* center of mass of a robot
* metabolic state of a cell
* operational state of an institution
* execution state of software
* knowledge state of a civilization

---

## 7. Organizational Trajectory

Organization is represented by a trajectory

```text
x_i(t)
```

moving through a viability region.

Persistence corresponds to continued existence of viable trajectories.

Organization is therefore dynamic rather than static.

---

## 8. Failure Boundary

The boundary of the viability region is

```text
∂P_i
```

Crossing the boundary constitutes organizational failure.

Examples:

* robot falls
* organism dies
* institution collapses
* software becomes unusable
* language becomes extinct

---

```text
D_i
```

```text
F_i
```

---

## 10. Persistence

Persistence occurs when

```text
x_i(t)
∈ P_i
```

for sufficient duration.

---

## 11. Stabilization

An abstraction stabilizes when its trajectory remains within its viability region despite perturbation.

---

# Part III: Viability Geometry

## 12. Viability

Viability measures distance from failure.

```text
V_i
=
distance(
x_i(t),
∂P_i
)
```

Higher viability implies greater resilience against perturbation.

---

## 13. Viability Reserve

Let

```text
T_i
```

represent the minimum viability threshold.

Then

```text
B_i
=
V_i - T_i
```

defines viability reserve.

Viability reserve measures available resilience capacity.

---

## 14. Collapse Tolerance

Let

```text
τ_i
```

represent allowable duration below threshold.

Collapse occurs when

```text
V_i < T_i
```

for longer than

```text
τ_i
```

---

## 15. Maintenance

Maintenance consists of actions that preserve trajectories inside viability regions.

```text
M_i
```

Examples:

* repair
* regulation
* homeostasis
* governance
* feedback control
* memory preservation

Maintenance continually redirects trajectories away from failure boundaries.

---

## 16. Degradation

Degradation consists of processes that move trajectories toward failure boundaries.

```text
D_i
```

Examples:

* entropy
* wear
* corruption
* institutional decay
* resource depletion

---

# Part IV: Affordances

## 17. Affordances

Affordances are viable future trajectories.

```text
F_i
```

Formally:

```text
F_i
=
{
future trajectories
remaining inside P_i
}
```

Affordances represent preserved future possibilities.

---

## 18. Affordance Capacity

Affordance capacity measures the volume of viable future trajectories available from the current state.

Large viability regions tend to preserve larger affordance spaces.

---

## 19. Reachability

```text
R_i
```

Measures whether an abstraction can be discovered, accessed, referenced, or utilized.

An affordance that cannot be reached is effectively lost.

---

# Part IV-A: Nested Viability and Recursive Constraint Satisfaction

## 19A. Sustaining Regions and Survival Regions

A viability region is not necessarily homogeneous.

Many abstractions possess both:

```text
Q_i
```

a sustaining region,

and

```text
U_i
```

a survival region,

where

```text
Q_i ⊂ U_i
```

The sustaining region contains states that actively replenish or maintain viability.

The survival region contains states in which viability may persist temporarily while reserves are consumed.

Examples:

Fish:

```text
water
→ sustaining region
```

```text
wet shoreline
→ survival region
```

Human:

```text
Earth surface environment
→ sustaining region
```

```text
high altitude vacuum exposure
→ survival region
```

An abstraction may remain viable outside its sustaining region for limited duration.

Persistence therefore depends not only upon location within a viability region but also upon available reserves.

---

## 19B. Viability Consumption

Outside sustaining regions viability may decrease over time.

Formally:

```text
dV_i/dt < 0
```

even while

```text
V_i > 0
```

The abstraction remains viable but is consuming viability reserve.

Examples:

* fish stranded on land
* astronaut without life support
* company operating at a loss
* civilization consuming strategic reserves

Temporary persistence is therefore possible despite movement outside sustaining conditions.

---

## 19C. Viability Replenishment

Within sustaining regions maintenance processes may restore viability.

Formally:

```text
dV_i/dt > 0
```

when maintenance exceeds degradation.

Examples:

* feeding
* repair
* healing
* infrastructure investment
* institutional reform

Sustaining regions permit viability replenishment.

Survival regions permit only viability expenditure.

---

## 19D. Viability Reserve Dynamics

Persistence depends on both viability and available reserve.

Let:

```text
B_i
=
V_i - T_i
```

represent viability reserve.

Temporary excursions outside sustaining regions are possible provided reserve remains positive.

Collapse occurs when reserve is exhausted.

This explains why organisms, institutions, and civilizations may survive temporarily under adverse conditions.

---

# Part IV-B: Constraint Geometry

## 19E. Constraint Sets

Every abstraction depends upon multiple simultaneous constraints.

Let:

```text
K_1,
K_2,
...
K_n
```

represent required constraint regions.

Examples for a fish:

```text
oxygen
temperature
food access
osmotic regulation
predator avoidance
```

Each constraint generates an admissible region.

---

## 19F. Composite Viability Region

The viability region of an abstraction is the intersection of all required constraint regions.

Formally:

```text
P_i
=
⋂ K_j
```

An abstraction remains viable only when all required constraints are simultaneously satisfied.

Failure of any essential constraint may destroy viability.

---

## 19G. Constraint Bottlenecks

The smallest constraint region frequently determines overall viability.

Examples:

* oxygen limitation
* memory limitation
* energy limitation
* bandwidth limitation

Constraint bottlenecks often dominate organizational dynamics.

---

## 19H. Constraint Expansion

Support structures may expand constraint regions.

Examples:

* gills expand oxygen access
* lungs expand habitat access
* refrigeration expands food viability
* writing expands memory persistence
* computation expands information processing

Support structures therefore enlarge viability regions by relaxing constraints.

# Part VII-A: Recursive Composition

## 31A. Composition as Constraint Satisfaction

Composition requires simultaneous viability of participating abstractions.

Suppose:

```text
A
```

and

```text
B
```

compose to form

```text
C
```

The viability region of

```text
C
```

requires:

* viability of A
* viability of B
* compatibility of interfaces
* satisfaction of composition constraints

---

## 31B. Emergent Viability Regions

Composition generates new viability regions.

Formally:

```text
P_C
⊂
P_A
∩
P_B
```

plus additional compatibility constraints.

The composed abstraction generally possesses a smaller admissible region than either constituent abstraction individually.

Composition therefore increases both capability and fragility.

---

## 31C. Interface Constraints

Interfaces create additional viability requirements.

Examples:

* communication protocols
* biological compatibility
* legal agreements
* software APIs
* social norms

Composition succeeds only when interface constraints remain satisfied.

---

## 31D. Hierarchical Viability

Higher-order abstractions depend upon viability of lower-order abstractions.

Examples:

```text

→ organism

organism
→ group

group
→ institution

institution
→ civilization
```

Each layer creates new viability regions while depending upon lower layers.

---

## 31E. Viability Cascades

Failure may propagate through dependency layers.

Formally:

```text
failure
→ support loss
→ viability reduction
→ additional failures
```

Examples:

* ecosystem collapse
* infrastructure failure
* financial contagion
* software dependency failure

Viability therefore propagates both upward and downward through support networks.

---

## 31F. Recursive Constraint Satisfaction Principle

All persistent abstractions are maintained through recursive constraint satisfaction.

Lower-level abstractions satisfy constraints generating viability regions for higher-level abstractions.

Higher-level abstractions emerge only when sufficient lower-level viability persists.

Complexity therefore consists of nested layers of simultaneously satisfied constraints.

Support structures generate viability regions.

Organization exists only within such regions.

---

## Principle 2: Trajectory Principle

Organization is a trajectory within a viability region rather than a static structure.

---

## Principle 3: Affordance Principle

Affordances are viable future trajectories.

Persistence without affordances contributes little accumulation.

---

## Principle 4: Maintenance Principle

Maintenance preserves trajectories within viability regions.

---

Viability regions depend upon supporting abstractions.

---

Support structures that generate large viability regions with low maintenance burden increase accumulation potential.

---

Persistent abstractions become generators of future viability regions.

* reuse

across all required supporting layers.

## Principle 11: Constraint Intersection Principle

The viability region of an abstraction is the intersection of all required constraint regions.

Support structures increase viability by expanding admissible constraint regions.

# General Dynamic Principle

Support structures generate and expand constraint regions.

Constraint intersections generate viability regions.

Organizational trajectories move through those regions.

Some regions replenish viability.

Other regions merely consume viability reserve.

Affordances correspond to viable future trajectories reachable through constraint-satisfying motion.

Composition generates higher-order abstractions through additional constraint satisfaction.

Persistent complexity emerges from recursively nested layers of viable trajectories whose supporting constraints remain simultaneously satisfied.

Open-ended accumulation requires continual expansion of viable future possibility space while maintaining the viability of all supporting layers upon which future possibilities depend.
